GaoDe

A multimodal architectural design game that turns a familiar CAD workspace into a playful self‑assessment studio. Students model with hand tracking, computer vision, and offline speech commands, then compare outcomes against iconic precedents.

What GaoDe is trying to solve

In many design studios, students learn concepts but struggle to apply them during form-making. GaoDe bridges that gap through an open‑ended serious game where there are no “right/wrong” answers. Instead, students explore alternatives, receive immediate feedback, and iterate until they are satisfied.

Open‑ended play

Encourages experimentation and reduces fear of failure while still supporting self‑assessment.

Natural interaction

Hands + voice reduce menu hunting and keep attention on spatial reasoning and proportion.

Precedent comparison

Five iconic projects provide a clear target for reflection without forcing a single solution path.

Core features

Multimodal input
  • Leap Motion for precise hand/finger tracking in 3D modeling.
  • Webcam + CNN for finger counting (save / finish gestures).
  • Offline speech for lightweight keyword commands (start and view navigation).
Studio-friendly workflow
  • Runs inside a familiar CAD environment (Rhino/Grasshopper).
  • Custom pop‑up UI for game selection and configuration.
  • Real‑time feedback overlays directly in the modeling viewport.

How the system works

GaoDe combines three sensing streams (hands, vision, voice) and routes them into a game engine built in Grasshopper. Each game reuses the same pipeline, but varies the interaction mapping to avoid repetitive gameplay.

Gesture camera control

Left fist anchors a reference; the right hand orbits to pan/rotate, and distance controls zoom.

Finger-count actions

Webcam-based finger counting is used for quick “save” and “finish” actions.

Offline voice commands

Keyword spotting enables start + view navigation without cloud connectivity.

Five game modules

GaoDe’s first release included five mini-games, each inspired by a distinct architectural logic. Students create forms with their hands, then compare their result to the precedent for self‑assessment.

1) Brick Country House (Mies van der Rohe)

A 3D grid supports ordered composition. Students place vertical blocks and interior walls based on palm orientation, stabilize with a pointer finger, and finish with an open‑hand gesture.

2) Fallingwater (Frank Lloyd Wright)

Final style is hidden during play to challenge visualization. Cubist elements emerge from palm orientation/length; a final “high‑five” transforms the composition into a Fallingwater‑like cantilevered logic.

3) Dancing House (Frank Gehry)

Focuses on soft curved forms. Two hands define top and waist; spacing between fingers changes the curvature. Saving uses a deliberate finger gesture to continue composing multiple buildings.

4) House III (Peter Eisenman)

Explores rule‑based transformations and estrangement through intersecting cubes. Students insert two cubes arbitrarily, compare variations, and iterate quickly.

5) Capsule Tower (Kisho Kurokawa)

Shifts attention to vertical composition. A randomized algorithm encourages rapid iteration while students steer the outcome through dynamic hand motion, switching between “continue” (fists) and “inspect/save” (open hands).
