GaoDe (Game of Design)

An open-ended architectural design serious game built inside a familiar CAD workflow, using multimodal natural interaction (hand gestures + vision + offline speech commands) to support self-assessment, motivation, and deeper understanding of the design process.

Serious game Open-ended learning Rhino + Grasshopper Leap Motion gestures Machine vision Offline speech commands 2020 prototype
Context: Architectural design studio (Pars University) Evaluation: 41 first-year students (Jan 2020) Publication: PMCID: PMC9096335 · PMID: 35578701

Project summary

GaoDe is an educational tool designed as a serious, open-ended game for architectural design. Instead of teaching through fixed right-or-wrong answers, GaoDe encourages students to explore design moves, reflect on their choices, and self-assess their outcomes. The game runs directly within a CAD environment and uses multimodal interaction (gesture recognition, machine vision, and offline speech recognition) to make the design process feel more natural, playful, and engaging.

The initial version includes five mini-games inspired by iconic buildings and architects (Mies van der Rohe, Frank Lloyd Wright, Frank Gehry, Peter Eisenman, and Kisho Kurokawa). Students can build their own version, compare their results to the reference architecture, and iterate without fear of being judged.

Goals

  • Bridge the gap between knowledge acquisition and knowledge application in design studio work.
  • Increase motivation and reduce the feeling of failure by framing practice as a self-driven game.
  • Support self-assessment through immediate feedback and comparison to real iconic buildings.
  • Make interaction natural by connecting hands, eyes, and mind through multimodal interfaces.
  • Keep it open-ended: no predefined “correct” solution, only exploration and refinement.

Key deliverables

  • Interactive game framework inside Rhino CAD (Grasshopper-based).
  • Multimodal interaction: Leap Motion gestures + webcam vision + offline speech commands.
  • Custom UI for game setup and real-time feedback during play.
  • Five game modes (iconic buildings) for guided self-assessment and comparison.
  • Classroom study + survey analysis of usability, engagement, learning impact, and satisfaction.

System architecture and toolchain

  • Design environment: Rhino CAD + Grasshopper (parametric scripting and real-time control).
  • Gesture interaction: Leap Motion Controller (precise hand and finger tracking for 3D modeling tasks).
  • Machine vision: webcam-based finger-count recognition to trigger save/finish states.
  • Speech recognition: lightweight offline speech-command model (keyword spotting) for simple commands.
  • UI layer: custom pop-up and in-scene real-time feedback to reduce friction and code exposure.
  • Outputs: digital 3D model in CAD, with the option to move toward physical output (e.g., 3D printing).

Multimodal interaction design

1) Hand gestures for modeling and navigation

GaoDe maps hand orientation, distance, and finger states to direct modeling actions. Gestures are designed to support fast iteration while reducing repeated menu operations typical of mouse-and-keyboard workflows.

  • Modeling: hand orientation and pinch/pose drive block creation and form control.
  • Camera control: orbit and zoom are mapped to two-hand relationships during play.

2) Vision-based finger counting for save and finish

A webcam + vision model recognizes finger count to trigger key states without interrupting flow. In the prototype, one finger is used for temporary save and five fingers for finishing the game.

3) Offline speech commands with a simple activation cue

Speech commands support quick actions (e.g., start, change views) without cloud reliance. Voice input is activated only when a yellow color cue is detected by the webcam.

4) Lightweight, readable game UI

A clean pop-up interface helps students choose a game mode, set difficulty, and understand the rules without touching Grasshopper code. Real-time visual feedback inside Rhino supports self-assessment during play.

Game modes (iconic buildings)

The first version of GaoDe includes five games. Each game emphasizes a different design skill (3D perception, compositional logic, curvilinear form, spatial estrangement, or vertical modularity) while keeping the experience open-ended and self-driven.

1) Mies van der Rohe · Brick Country House

Learning focus: ordered 3D composition and spatial perception through block-based design.

Interaction: use palm orientation to place walls/blocks; finger pose stabilizes elements; finish when satisfied.

2) Frank Lloyd Wright · Fallingwater

Learning focus: visualization and proportion without showing the final reference style during play.

Interaction: stack and arrange cubist elements; stabilize with finger pose; finalize to see Fallingwater-like outcome.

3) Frank Gehry · Dancing House

Learning focus: curvilinear, asymmetric form generation through finger-based control.

Interaction: two-hand finger spacing defines top/waist; specific finger pose saves the building and allows repetition.

4) Peter Eisenman · House III

Learning focus: exploration of rotation, overlap, and estrangement by inserting cubes at arbitrary angles.

Interaction: fist order and position drive cube creation; students evaluate the result from multiple viewpoints.

5) Kisho Kurokawa · Capsule Tower

Learning focus: vertical modular design, speed, and playful exploration with a semi-randomized algorithm.

Interaction: two fists drive vertical composition; open hands to pause and review from different perspectives, then continue.

Classroom study (Jan 2020)

GaoDe was examined in a real classroom context as part of a quantitative study in an architectural design studio. A 19-item questionnaire (five-point Likert scale) assessed usability, game mode evaluation, perceived learning improvement, and satisfaction.

  • Participants: 41 first-year architecture students (24 male, 17 female).
  • Instrument structure: 4 subsets (Usability, Evaluation of game modes, Improvement in learning, Satisfaction).
  • Overall trend: Strong positive responses across all four subsets; students also reported high willingness to recommend.

Highlights (mean ± SD)

  • User-friendly UI: 4.171 ± 0.729
  • Low mental effort: 4.049 ± 0.697
  • Self-assessment appropriateness: 4.122 ± 0.705
  • Enhanced knowledge: 4.317 ± 0.642
  • Overall satisfaction: 4.049 ± 0.764
  • Would suggest to peers: 4.634 ± 0.482

These numbers summarize the reported attitudes toward usability, engagement, and learning impact.

Contributions and outcomes

  • Proposed a serious open-ended game for architectural design that supports self-assessment.
  • Demonstrated an empirical implementation of multimodal HCI (gesture + vision + offline speech) inside a CAD workflow.
  • Provided survey-based evidence that students perceived GaoDe as easy to use, engaging, and supportive of learning.
  • Showed a practical pathway to reduce fear of failure by enabling students to iterate freely and compare outcomes to iconic references.
