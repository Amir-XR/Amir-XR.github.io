What GaoDe is trying to solve

In many design studios, students learn concepts but struggle to apply them during form-making. GaoDe bridges that gap through an open‚Äëended serious game where there are no ‚Äúright/wrong‚Äù answers. Instead, students explore alternatives, receive immediate feedback, and iterate until they are satisfied.

Open‚Äëended play

Encourages experimentation and reduces fear of failure while still supporting self‚Äëassessment.

Natural interaction

Hands + voice reduce menu hunting and keep attention on spatial reasoning and proportion.

Precedent comparison

Five iconic projects provide a clear target for reflection without forcing a single solution path.

  • Webcam + CNN for finger counting (save / finish gestures).
  • Offline speech for lightweight keyword commands (start and view navigation).

How the system works

GaoDe combines three sensing streams (hands, vision, voice) and routes them into a game engine built in Grasshopper. Each game reuses the same pipeline, but varies the interaction mapping to avoid repetitive gameplay.

Gesture camera control

Left fist anchors a reference; the right hand orbits to pan/rotate, and distance controls zoom.

Finger-count actions

Webcam-based finger counting is used for quick ‚Äúsave‚Äù and ‚Äúfinish‚Äù actions.

Offline voice commands

Keyword spotting enables start + view navigation without cloud connectivity.

Five game modules

GaoDe‚Äôs first release included five mini-games, each inspired by a distinct architectural logic. Students create forms with their hands, then compare their result to the precedent for self‚Äëassessment.

1) Brick Country House (Mies van der Rohe)

A 3D grid supports ordered composition. Students place vertical blocks and interior walls based on palm orientation, stabilize with a pointer finger, and finish with an open‚Äëhand gesture.

2) Fallingwater (Frank Lloyd Wright)

Final style is hidden during play to challenge visualization. Cubist elements emerge from palm orientation/length; a final ‚Äúhigh‚Äëfive‚Äù transforms the composition into a Fallingwater‚Äëlike cantilevered logic.

3) Dancing House (Frank Gehry)

Focuses on soft curved forms. Two hands define top and waist; spacing between fingers changes the curvature. Saving uses a deliberate finger gesture to continue composing multiple buildings.

4) House III (Peter Eisenman)

Explores rule‚Äëbased transformations and estrangement through intersecting cubes. Students insert two cubes arbitrarily, compare variations, and iterate quickly.

5) Capsule Tower (Kisho Kurokawa)

Shifts attention to vertical composition. A randomized algorithm encourages rapid iteration while students steer the outcome through dynamic hand motion, switching between ‚Äúcontinue‚Äù (fists) and ‚Äúinspect/save‚Äù (open hands).

User study and results (classroom deployment)

The tool was tested in an actual classroom setting (January 2020) with 41 first‚Äëyear architecture students in a mandatory design studio. A quantitative survey captured usability, motivation, and perceptions of learning support.

High acceptance
  • 80% liked using GaoDe as a learning tool.
  • 80% wanted to use it more in academic learning.
  • 75% found it useful for self‚Äëassessment.
  • 90% preferred physical interaction vs. traditional learning environments.
Top-rated aspects (1‚Äì5 Likert)

Physical interaction, hand control, and perceived 3D perception support were among the strongest scores. Speech commands scored slightly lower than hand-based interaction (still positive overall).
